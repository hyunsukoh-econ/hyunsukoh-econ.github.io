PhD Candidate in Economics · Iowa State University

I am a PhD candidate in economics at Iowa State University, expected to graduate in 2027. My research interests lie at the intersection of environmental economics and labor economics, with a focus on understanding how environmental conditions shape labor market outcomes and work arrangements. Specifically, I study the causal effects of environmental factors, such as variation in weather conditions, on work arrangement decisions across specific occupations and the broader workforce.

Job Market Paper

Cold Days, Warm Homes: The Impact of Winter Weather on Remote Work

Latest draft available upon request

Abstract

This study examines how winter weather conditions affect remote work patterns across U.S. metropolitan statistical areas. Combining individual-level telework data from the Current Population Survey with daily weather records from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration, I employ a two-way fixed effects model and a continuous treatment difference in differences framework to estimate the causal impact of winter weather on labor supply decisions and trace the dynamic dose- response relationship between snow depth and telework hours. The empirical re- sults demonstrate that adverse winter conditions, specifically lower temperatures and greater snow depth, significantly increase teleworking hours, with this effect markedly stronger in occupations characterized by high telework feasibility. Furthermore, while snowpack sharply reduces on-site work, telework partially compensates for the lost hours, mitigating the overall decline in total labor supply. The estimated dose-response curve confirms a robust, positive causal relationship between snow depth intensity and remote work substitution. These findings suggest that spatial reallocation of workplaces serves as an effective adaptation mechanism against weather-induced la- bor disruptions, offering important implications for climate adaptation policies and infrastructure planning.

Working Paper

Too Hot to Work? Heat Waves and Construction Laborers in the U.S.

Abstract

This study investigates the impact of heat waves on the working hours of construction laborers in U.S. metropolitan statistical areas (MSAs). Utilizing daily temperature data and working hours data, the research explores how extreme heat affects labor output in short-term heat shocks, contrasting with prior studies that often focus on long-term climate adaptation. Using a two-way fixed-effects model, this paper investigates the impact of increasing heat waves on construction laborers, accounting for both individual and temporal heterogeneity. The results suggest that under limited samples with a small amount of precipitation, an additional day per week with temperatures exceeding 90°F reduces working hours by 0.2 hours, and the occurrence of heat waves at least once a week decreases working hours by 0.7 hours. Despite the statistical significance of some results, implications must be drawn care- fully as the magnitudes are not large.
